1 Wakanda and Zakanda are two neighboring countries. Wakanda has a highly advanced technology, whereas, Zakanda has a lower level of technology and lower income per capita. In the usual setting, we would expect Zakanda to learn about Wakanda's technology but Wakanda has been successfully hiding its technology from the rest of the world for centuries. At time $t=0$, Wakanda has a productivity of 10, $A_w = 10$, and 20% of its labor force engage in R&D, $\gamma_{A,W} = .2$. Zakanda, on the other hand, has a productivity level of 6, $A_Z=6$, and 10% of its population engages in R&D, $\gamma_{A,Z}=.1$. Each country has a labor force equal to 1, $L=1$. Price of invention in each country is equal to 10, $\mu = 10$. Along the line, the following events take place:

  • After long soul searching, at time $t=10$, King T'Challa of Wakanda decides to open up Wakanda's technology to Zakanda.
  • King T'Chala observes Zakanda's development for 10 periods. But, he is surprised that Zakanda did not imitate Wakanda's technology and it grows at its former pace, as before time $t=10$. He appoints his sister and Chief Scientist of Wakanda, Shuri, as his technology ambassador to Zakanda at time $t=20$.
  • With the help of Shuri, Zakanda is able to imitate Wakanda's technology with a cost of copying equal to $\mu_{c} = \mu_i \left(\frac{A_W}{A_Z} \right)^{-1/2}.$ Shuri, who is highly intelligent and witty, convinces Zakanda to increase the fraction of workers engaged in R&D to 15%, $\gamma_{A,Z}=.15$
  • After observing the progress made since $t=20$, Zakandans increase the fraction of labor force engaging R&D to 22%, $\gamma_{A,Z}=.22 $ at time $t=50.$

a. Using the appropriate models we covered in Lecture (You need to employ one-country model of technology creation and growth and two-country model of technology creation and growth in answering this question.), simulate and plot Wakanda and Zakanda productivities and income per capita levels from $t=0$ to $t=250$ considering the events took place. Briefly comment on your graphs. In your graphs, make sure to highlight each event.

b. What might have prevented Zakanda from imitating Wakanda's technology from time $t=10$ to time $t=20$? Briefly explain. (In this question, I expect you to read section 8.4 (Barrier to international technology transfer) of Weil (2013).)

Solution¶

Notice that until $t=20$, Wakanda's technology is hidden from Zakanda. Hence, we need to consider technological progress in each of the countries separately as our one country model. Therefore, to calculate Wakanda's productivity and income over time, first we calculate productivity growth rate:

$$ \hat{A_w}(t) = \frac{\gamma_{A,w} L }{\mu}$$

Then, we calculate output:

$$ y_{w}(t) = A_w(t)(1-\gamma_{A,w}) $$

Then, using the growth rate we calculated and current productivity level, we can calculate next period's productivity:

$$ A_w(t+1) = A_w(t)(1+\hat{A_w}(t))$$

Starting from $t=0$, we repeat these calculations 20 times to get productivity and income of Wakanda for $t=0,20$

We calculate Zakanda's productivity and income until t=19 as we did for Wakanda:

$$ \hat{A_z}(t) = \frac{\gamma_{A,z} L }{\mu}$$$$ y_{z}(t) = A_z(t)(1-\gamma_{A,z}) $$$$ A_z(t+1) = A_z(t)(1+\hat{A_z}(t))$$

Repeat the above calculations 19 times.

Now, at time $t=20$, we observe 2 important developments. First, Zakanda begins imitating Wakanda's technology, and second, Zakanda increases share of labor force engaged in R&D to .15, notated as $\gamma_{A,z}^n$, where $n$ represents 'new'. However, as Wakanda is the technology leader, nothing changes for Wakanda, and we can keep calculating Wakanda's income and output as we did before until $t=50$. Now, Zakanda's productivity growth can be calculated as follows:

First, calculate the cost of imitation at time $t$, $$\mu_{c}(t) = \mu \left(\frac{A_w(t)}{A_z(t)} \right)^{-1/2}$$

Notice that cost of imitation depends on the ratio of productivities in these countries. Hence, we need to calcualte a new cost of imitation at each time $t$. Then we can calculate productivity growth rate in Zakanda,

$$ \hat{A_z}(t) = \frac{\gamma_{A,z}^n L }{\mu_c(t)}$$

Lastly, we can can calculate output in Zakanda,

$$ y_{z}(t) = A_z(t)(1-\gamma_{A,z}^n) $$

Lastly we need to repeat this from $t=20$ to $t=50$. At time $t=50$, Zakanda increases the faraction of labor force engaged in R&D to .22. Now, we repeat the above process with the most recent $\gamma_{A,z}$. This will go on for a while, until the point Zakanda's productivity surpases Wakanda's productivity. Then, Zakanda will become the technology leader. Therefore, after that time, cost of innovation in Zakanda will be equal to cost of invention, and cost of innovation in Wakanda will be equal to cost of copying.

$$\mu_{c}(t) = \mu \left(\frac{A_z(t)}{A_w(t)} \right)^{-1/2}$$$$ \hat{A_z}(t) = \frac{\gamma_{A,z}^n L }{\mu}$$$$ \hat{A_w}(t) = \frac{\gamma_{A,w}^n L }{\mu_c(t)}$$

We will iterate this process until $t=250.$

As you see above, we have many missing obsevations in the researcher data. We need to interpolate it. Say, for a country $i$, we hava data for years $t_1$ and $t_2$ with number of researchers equal to $y_{1}$ and $y_{2}$ respectively. Then for any $t_0$ such that $t_1 \leq t_0\leq t_2$, the interpolated number of researchers $y_0$ at time $t_0$ is equal to $$y_0 = y_1\frac{t_2-t_0}{t_2-t_1} + y_2\frac{t_0-t_1}{t_2-t_1}.$$

  • Don't interpolate the missing observations before the first available observation.
  • Don't interpolate the missing observations after the last available observation.

Answering part f is quite tricky. In our formula, we have $\hat{A} \equiv \frac{\dot{A}}{A} = \frac{L_A}{\mu}$. The question basically asks us whether $\mu$ is a constant or a function of technology $A$? We observe number of researchers in the data, hence $L_A$ is easy to find. However, we still don't know what $\frac{\dot{A}}{A}$ is. We can make one of these assumptions and conduct our analysis:

a. $\frac{\dot{A}}{A}$ is equal to the number of patents granted at each year. This basically a normalization as we don't what $A$ is. If we go with this assumption, for our formulate to hold, the number of patents over the number of researchers should be constant over time as $\frac{\frac{\dot{A}}{A}}{L_A} = {\mu}$

b. $\dot{A}$ is equal to the number of new patents. Then, what is A?

In this case, since the number of patents over the number of researchers are going down, we find evidence agianst $\mu$ is constant.

How should we analyze case b? Suppose $A(t)$ is the total number of patents granted until time t. Suppose our formulation is true, then $\frac{\dot{A}(t)}{A(t)}$ should increase as $L_A(t)$ increases in our data. For $\frac{\dot{A}(t)}{A(t)}$ to increase over time, number of new patents at each year should increase at an increasing rate. However, as shown the growth rate of patents, number of new patents are increasing at a decreasing rate.
